Module 8 Knowledge Checking

Use this module to review the entire workflow before applying it to a study dataset. The questions test concepts and decision points rather than memorization of settings that may vary by protocol.

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

  1. Describe the complete path from device deployment to an analysis-ready dataset.
  2. Identify decisions that must be study-specific and documented.
  3. Recognize when a record needs review rather than an automatic exclusion.

8.1 Course knowledge check

  1. What is the difference between a raw accelerometer signal and a derived activity outcome?
  2. Why should original device recordings be retained after conversion or processing?
  3. Name two protocol decisions that should be finalized before monitors are deployed.
  4. Why does device placement matter when interpreting accelerometer outcomes?
  5. What information should be recorded in a download or batch log?
  6. Why should GGIR arguments be documented before processing begins?
  7. What is the difference between a participant-level output and a day-level output?
  8. What is the purpose of visual inspection during quality control?
  9. Before appending two batches, what should you check?
  10. What should accompany a final analysis dataset so another analyst can understand how it was made?
Suggested answers
  1. The raw signal is the sensor time series; a derived outcome is an estimate generated after documented processing.
  2. They are the traceable source data and may be needed to verify or rerun later steps.
  3. Examples include wear location, schedule, participant instructions, sampling settings, and valid-data rules.
  4. Placement affects the signal and determines which processing methods and interpretations are appropriate.
  5. The study or device identifier, date, operator, input/output locations, and any issue or follow-up.
  6. The arguments determine how recordings are processed and must be reproducible and appropriate to the study.
  7. Participant-level outputs summarize one participant; day-level outputs contain separate observations for each participant-day.
  8. It identifies patterns or technical concerns that summary values alone may not reveal.
  9. Identifiers, variable definitions, units, observation levels, row counts, and batch documentation.
  10. A reproducible script or do-file, data dictionary, processing and quality-control logs, and the applicable protocol decisions.

8.2 Final reflection

Before beginning a real analysis, identify one decision from each module that your study team must approve or document. This is the practical habit that makes accelerometer research reproducible.
