Lesson 1.1

What Is an Accelerometer?

An accelerometer is a sensor that records acceleration over time, commonly along three axes. In human movement research, the device does not directly “know” whether a person is exercising, sitting, or asleep. Researchers combine the recorded signal with a documented processing method, a wear location, and a study protocol to derive interpretable outcomes.

The raw signal is commonly stored in gravitational units (g) or metres per second squared (m/s²). Later in the pipeline, the signal can be summarized into metrics such as ENMO (Euclidean Norm Minus One), a raw-acceleration metric that subtracts 1 g to remove the static effect of gravity and summarize dynamic movement. Intensity summaries such as MVPA are not direct sensor readings; they are derived after applying thresholds or algorithms to processed data.

Lesson 1.2

What Can Accelerometers Measure?

Accelerometer recordings can support estimates of several dimensions of movement behavior. The exact outcome depends on the device, placement, algorithm, calibration, and processing choices. Common outcomes include physical-activity volume and intensity, sedentary time, body posture, activity type, and sleep-wake timing.

Domain Examples of potential outcomes Interpretation note
Physical activity Movement volume, energy expenditure, steps, light activity, MVPA, vigorous activity Estimates are method-dependent; report the processing approach and thresholds.
Sedentary behavior Sedentary time, breaks, and accumulated sedentary bouts Posture-sensitive estimates require an appropriate placement and algorithm.
Sleep-wake behavior Sleep duration, onset, offset, midpoint, latency, efficiency, fragmentation These are algorithm-derived estimates, not a clinical diagnosis.
Body posture Sitting, standing, lying Usually depends on placement such as thigh or trunk sensors.
Activity type Walking, running, cycling, stair-stepping Activity-type classification is more algorithm-specific than volume and intensity summaries.

Lesson 1.3

Placement and Protocol Decisions

Before a monitor is initialized, the study team must choose the device model, wear location, start and stop dates, instructions for participants, and criteria for evaluating whether a recording is usable. These decisions affect participant burden and the outcomes that can be validly interpreted.

Different monitor placements are useful for different questions. A thigh-worn monitor may support posture classification, while wrist placement often improves comfort and continuous wear. The best placement follows the research question and protocol rather than a universal rule.

Quick Reference: Placement Planning

Placement Good fit when the study needs… Main planning risk
Wrist High compliance, 24-hour wear, broad activity/sleep workflows Intensity estimates and cut-points must match wrist placement.
Hip/waist Ambulatory activity volume and historical comparability Lower comfort and removal during sleep/bathing may reduce wear time.
Thigh Posture-sensitive outcomes such as sitting/standing/lying Attachment method and skin comfort must be planned carefully.
Chest/trunk Trunk acceleration and some gait/posture applications More obtrusive; not the default for many free-living studies.
Ankle Locomotion in infants/toddlers or populations where hip/wrist are limited Sleep vs sedentary separation may be difficult.
Low back Balance and some whole-body movement applications Adherence and belt attachment need protocol attention.
Evidence details: placement strengths and limitations
Placement Strengths Limitations
Wrist (Doherty et al., 2017) Large-scale wrist-worn protocols have been used successfully in population assessment. Participants may wear wrist-worn devices for longer periods than hip-worn devices (Kerr et al., 2017). Sometimes less accurate than hip placement for selected physical-activity estimates (Kerr et al., 2017).
Chest (Nazarahari & Rouhani, 2018) Chest-derived acceleration can reflect physical activity intensity and has been used to detect postures and walking modalities (Mukaino et al., 2022; Nazarahari & Rouhani, 2018). Trunk movement measurement with a chest-mounted accelerometer may be less easy to implement than wrist-worn protocols (Mukaino et al., 2022).
Low back (Williamson et al., 2026) Commonly used for balance assessment and whole-body movement applications (Ghislieri et al., 2019). Participant adherence can be challenging in skin-taped or body-worn protocols (Schneller et al., 2017).
Hip (Migueles et al., 2017) Hip placement has strong historical use and can reflect energy expenditure well in laboratory and locomotive activities (Hildebrand et al., 2014). Hip placement may be perceived as less comfortable and more socially obtrusive than wrist placement, lowering wear-time compliance in some groups (Scott et al., 2017).
Thigh (Kozey-Keadle et al., 2011) Thigh inclination can classify sitting more accurately than pelvis inclination, and thigh-based algorithms have been developed for activities such as stair walking and cycling (Peterson et al., 2015; Skotte et al., 2014). Attachment may require tape solutions that can loosen with activity, clothing changes, or participant discomfort (Arvidsson et al., 2019).
Ankle (Dorris et al., 2025) Can support continuous 24-hour collection and may capture locomotion when hip or wrist placement is limited (Campbell et al., 2021). May have difficulty differentiating sedentary time from sleep in young children (Hager et al., 2016).

Protocol review

Confirm decisions before recording.

Confirm the wear schedule, sampling frequency, valid-day criteria, non-wear algorithm, reporting units, and thresholds with the study’s principal investigator or data lead. Prior cohort protocols are examples, not defaults for every study.

Wrap-up

Knowledge Check

  1. What is the difference between a raw acceleration signal and an activity outcome?
  2. Why does device placement matter when interpreting accelerometer data?
  3. Name two study decisions that should be documented before devices are deployed.
Suggested answers
  1. The raw signal is a time series recorded by the sensor; an outcome is an estimate derived after processing the signal with documented methods.
  2. Placement changes the movement signal and determines which algorithms and interpretations are appropriate.
  3. Examples include wear location, wear schedule, initialization dates, participant instructions, sampling settings, and valid-data criteria.
