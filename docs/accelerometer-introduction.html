Module 1 Accelerometer Introduction

Accelerometry turns continuous movement signals into information about physical activity, sedentary behavior, and sleep-wake patterns. This module gives the conceptual foundation for the workflow taught in the rest of the course.

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

  1. Explain what an accelerometer records and how raw signals become research outcomes.
  2. Distinguish common accelerometer-derived physical activity, sedentary-behavior, and sleep-wake measures.
  3. Describe how device placement and study protocol shape the data that can be collected and interpreted.

1.1 Lesson 1.1: What is an accelerometer?

An accelerometer is a sensor that records changes in acceleration over time, typically along three axes. In human research, the device records movement-related signals that can be processed into interpretable summaries. The raw signal is commonly stored in gravitational units (g) or metres per second squared (m/s²).

The device does not directly “know” whether a person is exercising, sitting, or asleep. Researchers combine the recorded signal with a clearly documented processing method, device placement, and study protocol to estimate these outcomes.

1.1.1 From device to dataset

  1. Plan the study protocol and deploy the monitor.
  2. Download the raw recording after the wear period.
  3. Organize and, if needed, convert files for analysis.
  4. Process raw data with an established workflow, such as GGIR.
  5. Inspect data quality and document exclusions or concerns.
  6. Derive outcomes and create an analysis-ready dataset.

1.2 Lesson 1.2: What can accelerometers measure?

Accelerometer recordings can support estimates of several dimensions of movement behavior. The exact outcome depends on the device, placement, algorithm, calibration, and processing choices.

Domain Examples of potential outcomes Interpretation note
Physical activity Movement volume, intensity distribution, light activity, moderate-to-vigorous activity, steps Estimates are method-dependent; report the processing approach.
Sedentary behavior Sedentary time, breaks, and accumulated sedentary bouts Posture-sensitive estimates require an appropriate device placement and algorithm.
Sleep-wake behavior Sleep timing, duration, midpoint, and fragmentation-related indicators These are algorithm-derived estimates, not a clinical diagnosis.

Different monitor placements can be useful for different questions. For example, a thigh-worn monitor may better support posture classification, while wrist placement often improves wear comfort and continuous data collection. The best placement follows the research question and protocol—not a universal rule.

1.3 Lesson 1.3: Placement and protocol decisions

Before a monitor is initialized, the study team must choose the device model, wear location, start and stop dates, instructions for participants, and criteria for evaluating whether a recording is usable. These decisions affect both participant burden and the outcomes that can be validly interpreted.

Placement Potential advantage Planning consideration
Wrist Often comfortable and compatible with continuous wear Algorithms and cut-points must match wrist placement.
Hip Common for ambulatory activity measurement Participants may remove it more often.
Thigh Can support posture-related classification Attachment and participant comfort need careful planning.
Ankle Can capture locomotor movement well in some designs It may be less suited to certain posture or sleep questions.

Protocol review required before recording: Confirm the wear schedule, sampling frequency, valid-day criteria, non-wear algorithm, reporting units, and thresholds with the study’s principal investigator or data lead. The values in prior cohort protocols are examples, not defaults for every study.

1.5 Knowledge check

  1. What is the difference between a raw acceleration signal and an activity outcome?
  2. Why does device placement matter when interpreting accelerometer data?
  3. Name two study decisions that should be documented before devices are deployed.
Suggested answers
  1. The raw signal is a time series recorded by the sensor; an outcome is an estimate derived after processing the signal with documented methods.
  2. Placement changes the movement signal and determines which algorithms and interpretations are appropriate.
  3. Examples include wear location, wear schedule, initialization dates, participant instructions, sampling settings, and valid-data criteria.
