Module 1 Accelerometer Introduction

Accelerometry turns continuous movement signals into information about physical activity, sedentary behavior, and sleep-wake patterns. This module gives the conceptual foundation for the workflow taught in the rest of the course: what an accelerometer records, what it can estimate, why placement matters, and how a study moves from a device on a participant to a clean analysis dataset.

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

  1. Explain what an accelerometer records and how raw signals become research outcomes.
  2. Distinguish common accelerometer-derived physical activity, sedentary-behavior, body-posture, activity-type, and sleep-wake measures.
  3. Describe how device placement and protocol choices shape the data that can be collected and interpreted.
  4. Read placement and device tables as planning tools rather than universal rules.

1.1 Lesson 1.1: What is an accelerometer?

An accelerometer is a sensor that records acceleration over time, commonly along three axes. In human movement research, the device does not directly “know” whether a person is exercising, sitting, or asleep. Researchers combine the recorded signal with a documented processing method, a wear location, and a study protocol to derive interpretable outcomes.

The raw signal is commonly stored in gravitational units (g) or metres per second squared (m/s²). Later in the pipeline, the signal can be summarized into metrics such as ENMO (Euclidean Norm Minus One), a raw-acceleration metric that subtracts 1 g to remove the static effect of gravity and summarize dynamic movement. Intensity summaries such as MVPA (moderate-to-vigorous physical activity) are not direct sensor readings; they are derived after applying thresholds or algorithms to processed data.

1.1.1 From device to dataset

The complete pipeline has many decisions, but the big picture is simple:

1Plan and deploy

Choose device, placement, sampling, wear protocol, and participant instructions.

2Download

Retrieve raw files and document return status, dates, and operator decisions.

3Organize and convert

Use reproducible folders, IDs, logs, and only convert formats when the pipeline needs it.

4Process

Run GGIR or another approved workflow to calibrate, detect non-wear, and summarize movement.

5Inspect quality

Review reports, plots, missingness, calibration error, clipping, and valid-day status.

6Clean and analyse

Apply documented rules, derive outcomes, prepare the final Stata dataset, and report decisions.

1.2 Lesson 1.2: What can accelerometers measure?

Accelerometer recordings can support estimates of several dimensions of movement behavior. The exact outcome depends on the device, placement, algorithm, calibration, and processing choices. Common outcomes include physical-activity volume and intensity, sedentary time, body posture, activity type, and sleep-wake timing Quante et al., 2015; Arvidsson et al., 2019.

Domain Examples of potential outcomes Interpretation note
Physical activity Movement volume, energy expenditure, steps, light activity, MVPA, vigorous activity Estimates are method-dependent; report the processing approach and thresholds.
Sedentary behavior Sedentary time, breaks, and accumulated sedentary bouts Posture-sensitive estimates require an appropriate placement and algorithm.
Sleep-wake behavior Sleep duration, onset, offset, midpoint, latency, efficiency, fragmentation These are algorithm-derived estimates, not a clinical diagnosis.
Body posture Sitting, standing, lying Usually depends on placement such as thigh or trunk sensors.
Activity type Walking, running, cycling, stair-stepping Activity-type classification is more algorithm-specific than volume/intensity summaries.

1.3 Lesson 1.3: Placement and protocol decisions

Before a monitor is initialized, the study team must choose the device model, wear location, start and stop dates, instructions for participants, and criteria for evaluating whether a recording is usable. These decisions affect participant burden and the outcomes that can be validly interpreted.

Different monitor placements are useful for different questions. A thigh-worn monitor may support posture classification, while wrist placement often improves comfort and continuous wear. The best placement follows the research question and protocol rather than a universal rule Migueles et al., 2017; Kerr et al., 2017.

1.3.1 Quick reference: placement planning

Placement Good fit when the study needs... Main planning risk
Wrist High compliance, 24-hour wear, broad activity/sleep workflows Intensity estimates and cut-points must match wrist placement.
Hip/waist Ambulatory activity volume and historical comparability Lower comfort and removal during sleep/bathing may reduce wear time.
Thigh Posture-sensitive outcomes such as sitting/standing/lying Attachment method and skin comfort must be planned carefully.
Chest/trunk Trunk acceleration and some gait/posture applications More obtrusive; not the default for many free-living studies.
Ankle Locomotion in infants/toddlers or populations where hip/wrist are limited Sleep vs sedentary separation may be difficult.
Low back Balance and some whole-body movement applications Adherence and belt attachment need protocol attention.

1.3.2 Evidence details: placement strengths and limitations

Protocol review required before recording: Confirm the wear schedule, sampling frequency, valid-day criteria, non-wear algorithm, reporting units, and thresholds with the study's principal investigator or data lead. Prior cohort protocols are examples, not defaults for every study.

1.5 Knowledge check

  1. What is the difference between a raw acceleration signal and an activity outcome?
  2. Why does device placement matter when interpreting accelerometer data?
  3. Name two study decisions that should be documented before devices are deployed.
Suggested answers
  1. The raw signal is a time series recorded by the sensor; an outcome is an estimate derived after processing the signal with documented methods.
  2. Placement changes the movement signal and determines which algorithms and interpretations are appropriate.
  3. Examples include wear location, wear schedule, initialization dates, participant instructions, sampling settings, and valid-data criteria.
